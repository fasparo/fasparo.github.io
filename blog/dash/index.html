A dash is a dash

2013-03-03

Many conventions of typewriter antiquity have survived into the computer age. I can see one that remains logical: the use of "dead keys" to get diacritics (accents) onto characters. Most are the product of now-forgotten limitations, and are slowly dying out: underlining and caps instead of italics, first-line indents instead of proper paragraphs, tabbing in general. Proper smart quotes at last replace dumb ones, albeit with a bit of software help. (By the way, the modern equivalent of yesterday's physical typewriter constraint is plain text, and it has a modern solution: Markdown.)

Alas, the worst limitation of all was set in stone when modern keyboard-makers forgot to include a key ‐ the dash. Or rather, a key â€“ the dash. So a generation of non-typographers has been forced to remember obscure key combinations (Alt+0150 on Windows, Cmd+- on Mac, Ctrl+Alt+U 2013 on Nix), or rely on Microsoft Word's conversion, or type two hyphens (the typewriter hack), or more likely just stuff it all and pretend the hyphen (which is already doubling as a minus) is a dash. A pity.
