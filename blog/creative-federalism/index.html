Creative federalism

2013-10-10

Is it possible to be "united in diversity"? The EU's motto expresses the ambiguity at the heart of federalism, and so fuels the fear that underlies EU-scepticism.

The fear looks reasonable. As is implied by the ominous term "European integration", the sharing of laws is likely to make Europe more integrated, or "whole". In general, states which pool sovereignty are destined to lose parts of their historic characters.

But they are not destined to become one state. Federalism demands nothing more than respect for a treaty.

Perhaps the key is to understand "diversity" in its literal, rather than historical, sense. An ideal federation is not a union, but a community of best practice. Each state pioneers its own laws, and occasionally the best laws are generalized to the other states. Only a state which never asserts its difference, which never tries anything new, need fear losing its identity.

So a federation can indeed be united in diversity. But only if that diversity is invented and created, not inherited. Perhaps that is why federalism is scary to Europe.
